Dear Hiring Team:

I am applying for the Legal Tech & AI Training Lead role because it is precisely the work I have been doing inside law firms: training legal teams to make practical use of new technology, coaching them through the change, and making sure adoption is real rather than merely announced.

My background is in litigation operations, but the work has always been broader than keeping cases organized. I have spent more than a decade inside plaintiff-side, corporate, and medical-malpractice practices helping teams manage the systems and processes that keep a firm moving: case management, discovery, docketing, document organization, trial preparation, and the day-to-day coordination behind active matters. I have worked through repeated technology changes from inside the firm, where success depends less on the software itself than on whether busy attorneys, paralegals, case managers, and staff can see a genuine improvement in their work.

Most recently, as a Senior Litigation Paralegal, I serve on my firm’s AI committee and enterprise AI pilot, where much of my role has been training and onboarding. I have helped introduce tools including Microsoft Copilot and Filevine’s Lois into real legal workflows — delivering hands-on sessions, coaching colleagues one-on-one at their desks, reviewing outputs against source material, and helping people understand where AI can assist and where professional judgment must remain central. I have trained users across every role and documented the practical conditions that turn a first use into a repeatable habit.

What appeals to me about this role is that it is fundamentally about people, not just tools. The posting describes delivering training on generative AI, onboarding new staff, and offering personalized coaching — and that is exactly the work I already do. I am comfortable standing in front of a room and sitting beside one person: building role-specific exercises tied to real work, and creating the documentation, job aids, and feedback loops that make a new skill stick.

My graduate training in biomedical visualization has shaped how I approach that enablement work. Much of my academic and professional work has involved translating technical scientific and medical information for audiences with very different levels of background knowledge, including attorneys, experts, students, clients, and lay decision-makers. Through formal coursework and project work in learning theory, instructional design, and scientific communication, I have learned to begin with the audience’s existing knowledge, identify the decision or skill they need to develop, and design materials that make complex information usable rather than simply more detailed.

That translates directly to AI training within a law firm. A firm is not one audience. It includes partners who need to understand strategic value, risk, and client implications; associates who may be looking for stronger ways to research, draft, and analyze; paralegals and case managers who carry much of the volume work; and administrative staff who need a clear understanding of how a new workflow changes their role. Those groups bring different levels of technical comfort, different concerns, and different reasons to engage. Effective adoption cannot rely on one broad training session or a generic product demonstration. It requires role-specific examples, practical exercises tied to real work, clear guardrails, and communication that helps each person understand both what the technology can do and what remains their responsibility.

I am a daily user of Microsoft 365 and Copilot, along with Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, and NotebookLM, and I bring full-stack coding foundations as well as a practical understanding of legal workflows and legal risk. This fall, I will begin a part-time evening Executive J.D. focused on AI law and governance — which will continue to sharpen my grasp of responsible-AI standards while I remain fully available for full-time work.

For this application, I have built a short work sample centered on a responsible-AI training and adoption program. It includes a Chicago pilot kickoff deck, an interactive training module, an adoption tracker, and a 90-day rollout plan. Together, those materials reflect how I approach this work: start with a real workflow, teach it clearly, build trust through verification, measure what changes, and scale once people are confident.

Thank you for your consideration. I would welcome the opportunity to discuss how my experience in litigation operations, legal-AI training and enablement, scientific communication, and hands-on coaching could support Willkie Farr & Gallagher’s legal-technology team.
