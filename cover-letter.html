Dear Hiring Team:

I am applying for the Knowledge & Innovation Program Manager role because it sits directly at the intersection of the work I have been doing inside law firms: helping legal teams make practical use of new technology, improving the workflows around it, and ensuring that adoption is real rather than merely announced.

My background is in litigation operations, but the work has always been broader than keeping cases organized. I have spent more than a decade inside plaintiff-side, corporate, and medical-malpractice practices helping teams manage the systems and processes that keep a firm moving: case management, discovery, docketing, document organization, trial preparation, and the day-to-day coordination behind active matters. I have worked through repeated technology changes from inside the firm, where success depends less on the software itself than on whether busy attorneys, paralegals, case managers, and staff can see a genuine improvement in their work.

Most recently, as a Senior Litigation Paralegal, I serve on my firm’s AI committee and enterprise AI pilot. I have helped evaluate and introduce tools including Microsoft Copilot and Filevine’s Lois into real legal workflows. That has meant translating business needs into specific use cases, reviewing outputs against source material, thinking carefully about confidentiality and privilege, and helping colleagues understand where AI can assist and where professional judgment must remain central. I have also helped train users across roles and document the practical conditions that turn a first use into a repeatable habit.

What appeals to me about this role is that it requires both program thinking and close attention to the work itself. I am comfortable identifying a workflow worth improving, defining where an AI-assisted process should begin, establishing a baseline, and measuring whether the change improves time, effort, quality, or client value. I am equally comfortable helping people use that workflow through guided practice, clear documentation, role-specific examples, and feedback loops.

My graduate training in biomedical visualization has shaped how I approach that enablement work. Much of my academic and professional work has involved translating technical scientific and medical information for audiences with very different levels of background knowledge, including attorneys, experts, students, clients, and lay decision-makers. Through formal coursework and project work in learning theory, instructional design, and scientific communication, I have learned to begin with the audience’s existing knowledge, identify the decision or skill they need to develop, and design materials that make complex information usable rather than simply more detailed.

That translates directly to AI adoption within a law firm. A firm is not one audience. It includes partners who need to understand strategic value, risk, and client implications; associates who may be looking for stronger ways to research, draft, and analyze; paralegals and case managers who carry much of the volume work; and administrative staff who need a clear understanding of how a new workflow changes their role. Those groups bring different levels of technical comfort, different concerns, and different reasons to engage. Effective adoption cannot rely on one broad training session or a generic product demonstration. It requires role-specific examples, practical exercises tied to real work, clear guardrails, and communication that helps each person understand both what the technology can do and what remains their responsibility.

I am a daily user of Copilot, Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, and NotebookLM, and I bring full-stack coding foundations as well as a practical understanding of legal workflows and legal risk. This fall, I will begin a part-time evening Executive J.D. focused on AI law and governance. The program will allow me to continue developing my understanding of responsible-AI standards while remaining fully available for full-time work.

For this application, I have built a short work sample centered on a responsible-AI adoption program. It includes a Chicago pilot deck, an interactive training module, an adoption-and-value tracker, and a 90-day implementation plan. Together, those materials reflect how I think about this work: begin with a real workflow, test it carefully, measure what changes, build trust through verification, and scale only once the value is clear.

Thank you for your consideration. I would welcome the opportunity to discuss how my experience in litigation operations, legal-AI adoption, scientific communication, and practical enablement could support Ashurst Perkins Coie’s Knowledge & Innovation team.
