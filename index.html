The approach

Responsible adoption, by design

Getting a firm to use AI is not a tool rollout — it is behavior change, earned safely. Four principles guide the work, and the last one is non-negotiable in a law firm.

01

Meet the work

Learn how each practice group and business team actually works before recommending where AI fits.

02

Enable every role

Role-based pathways for attorneys, associates, paralegals, and business professionals — because each adopts differently.

03

Guardrails first

Confidentiality, data classification, human-in-the-loop review, and citing sources — built into every workflow, not bolted on.

04

Measure value and risk

Track adoption, confidence, and where people still need coaching — and watch for misuse, bias, and hallucination, escalating risk signals early.

Why this work

Why legal AI, and why Willkie Farr & Gallagher

I have spent my career inside law firms because I have always been interested in how legal work can be made clearer, more effective, and more responsive to the people it serves. Long before AI became the center of that conversation, I was drawn to the practical work of helping firms improve the systems behind their cases: the processes, technologies, information flows, and habits that determine whether good legal judgment can actually move through an organization.

That interest is what led me to pursue graduate training in biomedical visualization, scientific communication, and learning design. I wanted a more technical understanding of how complex information can be translated, how people learn new skills, and how better tools can improve the way professionals make decisions and communicate with one another. At the time, I saw the greatest opportunity for innovation in law firms in the development of better systems for organizing information, communicating complex evidence, and helping teams work more effectively across roles.

My thinking has evolved as the legal ecosystem has begun to move toward more capable AI and agentic workflows. I still believe that better information design, stronger systems, and more thoughtful training matter enormously — but I now see responsible AI adoption as one of the most meaningful ways to advance innovation in law firms. The opportunity is not simply to introduce new tools. It is to help lawyers and staff use those tools to reduce friction, improve quality, and create more time for judgment, advocacy, and client care, without compromising confidentiality, accuracy, or professional responsibility.

Having been the person inside a firm doing this work, I know that adoption lives or dies on trust. People do not change their habits because a tool is impressive in a demonstration. They change when they understand where it fits into their work, can verify the output, see a real improvement in the task in front of them, and trust that the guardrails protect both them and their clients.

That is why the Legal Tech & AI Training Lead role is so compelling to me. It brings together legal practice, technology, and learning to help a firm’s people actually adopt AI — well, safely, and with real confidence. It also reflects the kind of work I do best: working across functions, understanding the needs of different users, building practical workflows, helping people develop confidence with new tools, and showing through evidence whether a change is actually improving the work.

I would bring firm-operations experience, formal training in learning theory and scientific communication, technical fluency, and hands-on AI-enablement experience to help Willkie Farr & Gallagher turn AI into measurable, defensible value for its lawyers and clients. My goal would not be adoption for its own sake — it would be to help the firm build responsible, useful, and durable ways of working that make the practice of law better.
