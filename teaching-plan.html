Sample plan · Teaching & enablement approach

How I’d teach the firm’s attorneys and staff

Attorneys are time-poor, results-driven, and rightly skeptical until something proves itself. Staff keep the firm running and feel every workflow change first. Teaching that works meets each of them inside their own work, proves value quickly, and builds trust through verification — never a one-size lecture. Below is how I would deliver on each part of this role, mapped to the responsibilities as written, followed by what the first ninety days would look like.

About this plan

A concrete delivery plan built around the posted responsibilities. Tool names (Harvey, CoCounsel, Wendell, Microsoft 365) follow the description; a first task on the job would be learning the exact configuration and guardrails of the firm’s own stack.

Five things stay constant: meet the workflow, teach each role differently, put the guardrails first, measure adoption and confidence — and make every answer easy to find again.

01 · AI & Innovation Enablement

From “what is this?” to confident, responsible use

Attorneys don’t need an AI lecture; they need to know what it does for the task in front of them and where the line is. I start with judgment and guardrails, then make the tools concrete.

A

Foundational AI literacy

A short, jargon-free session on what generative AI is and isn’t, how it can be confidently wrong, and the confidentiality, privilege, and verification rules that govern its use — paired with a one-page responsible-use guide.

B

The right tool for the task

Introduce Harvey, CoCounsel, and Wendell not as feature tours but mapped to real work — which tool for drafting, which for research and review, which for the firm’s specific workflows — demonstrated in each group’s own kind of matter.

C

Stay current, curate for the firm

A standing habit of tracking releases, vendor briefings, and bar technology developments; a quarterly “what’s new and what it means for us” note; and a curriculum that adjusts as the tools change.

02 · Core Technology Training & Onboarding

The everyday systems, taught to stick

The core platforms are where most time is quietly won or lost. Training here is hands-on, role-based, and short enough to fit inside a real working day.

A

Live sessions, in the real tools

In-person and virtual sessions on Microsoft 365, document management, communication platforms, and legal-tech tools — practical and task-based, and frequent rather than rare.

B

Role-based onboarding

A first-week technology path customized to each role, from new attorneys to business staff: guided setup, a clear checklist, and a 30-day follow-up so nothing is lost after day one.

C

Rollout & upgrade support

A repeatable playbook for firmwide software rollouts and major upgrades: pre-launch communication, champion prep, launch-day floor support, office hours, and a feedback loop that catches friction early.

03 · Personalized Coaching & Office Hours

Where confidence actually takes root

Group training plants the seed; one-on-one help is where it takes root. Much of real adoption happens at a single desk, with a single question.

A

Tech Office Hours

Predictable, recurring drop-in support — in-person and remote, a room and a video link — where anyone can bring the problem in front of them. Every question logged to reveal patterns.

B

1:1 coaching & refreshers

Booked, workflow-specific sessions for people who want a targeted refresher or help with a particular tool, with short follow-up notes they can keep.

C

A trusted guide through the change

Meeting hesitation with patience, normalizing the learning curve, and turning small wins into momentum — especially for those adapting to generative-AI tools and modern work platforms.

04 · Just-in-Time Learning & Content Development

The answer, ready at the moment of need

People learn when they hit a wall, not on a training calendar. The content has to be there when the question is — short, specific, and findable.

A

Bite-size, findable content

Tip sheets, brief walkthroughs, and short video snippets built for the moment of need — task-specific, skimmable, and easy to locate.

B

A living resource library

A single, searchable home for learning content — maintained and enhanced as tools change, with stale material retired rather than left to mislead.

C

Built from real use cases

Materials drawn from actual questions and real matters — especially for AI and automation tools — rather than generic vendor decks.

05 · Needs Analysis & Collaboration

Teaching as a feedback loop, not a broadcast

The best training program listens harder than it talks. I’d watch where people struggle and route what I learn to the people who can act on it.

Signals Spot the gaps early

Office-hours logs, session feedback, Help Desk ticket themes, and adoption metrics combined into a running view of where skills gaps and emerging needs are showing up — so training gets ahead of problems instead of reacting to them.

Partners Coordinate, don’t duplicate

Clear handoffs with the Help Desk, the Practice Innovation Center of Excellence, and Regional Advisors — so training complements innovation enablement rather than overlapping it, on a shared calendar and roadmap.

Loop Close the loop with product & engineering

Structured user feedback — what’s confusing, what’s missing, what to build next — shared back with internal product and engineering teams so the tools keep improving for the people who use them.

The first 90 days

How it comes together

A deliberate ramp: understand the firm before changing it, build and pilot on real needs, then scale with evidence.

1–30

Listen & learn

Meet the practice groups and business teams; learn the firm’s exact stack — including Harvey, CoCounsel, and Wendell — and its guardrails; shadow the Help Desk; audit the existing learning library; and identify the top pain points. Quick win: start Tech Office Hours and publish the two or three most-requested tip sheets.

31–60

Build & pilot

Launch the foundational AI-literacy session; stand up a role-based onboarding path; settle the Office Hours cadence; build a first set of just-in-time content from real questions; and begin tracking feedback and adoption.

61–90

Scale & measure

A full, published training calendar; onboarding live for new joiners; a reorganized, searchable content library; the first “what’s new in AI” briefing; and a needs-analysis summary for the CoE with the gaps I’ve observed and what I’d do next.
